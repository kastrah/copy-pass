# Copyblogger 5P Approach — Framework Summary

**Source:** Copyblogger, *The 5P Approach to Copy That Crushes It*.

Reusable framework for persuasive copy built around premise-driven storytelling.

## Core Idea

Great copy is not just a list of benefits. It is a story the prospect begins telling themselves. The copywriter’s job is to create a persuasive premise, express a bold promise, paint a vivid picture, prove the claim, and push for action.

## Premise Before Copy

A **premise** is the central concept or story angle behind the message. It shapes how the prospect understands the offer.

A strong premise should be:

### 1. Unpredictable
It must interrupt expectation. Study what the audience is used to seeing, then find a fresh angle, overlooked fact, or surprising frame.

Prompt: “What does the market expect me to say — and what true thing can I say instead?”

### 2. Simple
A big idea must be easy to grasp. Reduce the concept from a paragraph to two sentences to one clear line.

Prompt: “Can the prospect repeat the core idea after one read?”

### 3. Real
The premise must be relevant, meaningful, and tangible. Replace abstractions with concrete expressions of benefit.

Example pattern:
- Abstract: “More nutritious.”
- Tangible: “One bowl gives what would take many bowls of ordinary cereal.”

### 4. Credible
The premise should trigger desire emotionally, but still survive logical scrutiny. If it feels too good to be true, add proof, specificity, or a more believable mechanism.

## The 5P Copy Framework

### 1. Premise
Open with the controlling idea that makes the message interesting and meaningful.

Checklist:
- Is the angle fresh in this market?
- Does it connect to an existing belief or desire?
- Does it contain tension, curiosity, or contrast?
- Can it carry the whole piece from headline to CTA?

### 2. Promise
Turn the premise into a bold, desirable promise. The promise is often expressed in the headline or lead.

Good promises are:
- Specific enough to be believable
- Desirable enough to earn attention
- Connected to the premise
- Focused on the prospect’s outcome

Pattern: **Because [premise], you can get [desired result] without [common pain].**

### 3. Picture
Paint the emotional and sensory scene of the promised outcome. Use storytelling, images, contrast, and tangible language so the prospect imagines themselves benefiting.

Checklist:
- Show the before/after contrast.
- Make the desired outcome visible.
- Let the reader infer part of the story themselves.
- Tie features to lived benefits.
- Use concrete scenes instead of abstract claims.

Example story structure:
- Two similar people start in the same place.
- One gains a key advantage.
- Their outcomes diverge.
- The offer explains the difference.

### 4. Proof
Support the emotional decision with logic and evidence.

Proof assets:
- Statistics and studies
- Demonstrations
- Testimonials
- Case examples
- Third-party facts
- Charts or comparisons
- Product features that substantiate benefits
- Credentials or authority
- Guarantee or risk reversal

Proof should reinforce the premise rather than feel bolted on.

### 5. Push
Ask for action clearly. The push expands desire, resolves final hesitation, and gives the reader a simple next step.

Checklist:
- Restate the core benefit.
- Summarize the strongest proof.
- Remove risk or confusion.
- Add ethical urgency if available.
- Tell the reader exactly what to do.

Pattern: **If you want [benefit], take [specific action] now because [reason to act].**

## 5P Page Outline

1. Headline: promise rooted in the premise.
2. Lead: introduce the unexpected angle.
3. Problem: describe the current pain or missed opportunity.
4. Picture: show the desired future and emotional payoff.
5. Mechanism: explain why the offer makes the outcome possible.
6. Proof: evidence, examples, testimonials, demonstration.
7. Offer: what the reader gets.
8. Risk reversal: guarantee, reassurance, clarity.
9. Push: CTA with urgency and next step.

## Editing Questions

- Does one “golden thread” connect the headline, story, proof, and CTA?
- Is the premise fresh or is it a category cliché?
- Is the promise bold but believable?
- Can the reader picture the benefit?
- Is the proof strong enough for a skeptical buyer?
- Is the action step unmistakable?
