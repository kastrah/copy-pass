# A Technique for Producing Ideas — Framework Summary

**Source:** James Webb Young, *A Technique for Producing Ideas*

Reusable idea-generation principles and copywriting research process.

## Core Principles

### 1. Ideas Are New Combinations
An idea is a new combination of existing elements. Creative work is not magic; it is recombining known facts, observations, experiences, and patterns into a fresh relationship.

### 2. Relationship-Seeing Is the Creative Skill
The ability to make new combinations depends on the ability to see relationships between facts. Train yourself to ask:
- What else is this like?
- What broader law or pattern does this illustrate?
- What unrelated field has a useful analogy?
- What overlooked connection could make this familiar thing feel new?

### 3. Specific + General Material
Good advertising ideas come from combining:
- **Specific material:** deep knowledge of the product, market, buyers, objections, usage context, and lived customer experience.
- **General material:** broad knowledge of culture, psychology, history, science, art, behavior, news, and everyday life.

No browsing, no milk: the richer the mental storehouse, the more possible combinations.

## The Five-Step Idea Production Process

### Step 1 — Gather Raw Material
Collect facts systematically before looking for inspiration.

Checklist:
- Product facts: features, ingredients, process, proof, origin, differences.
- Customer facts: desires, frustrations, language, habits, fears, beliefs.
- Competitive facts: claims, angles, clichés, gaps.
- Context facts: when/where/how the product is used.
- General facts: analogies, stories, symbols, cultural references.

### Step 2 — Digest the Material
Work the facts over mentally. Sort, compare, group, question, and look for relationships.

Prompts:
- Which fact is most surprising?
- Which customer belief does this connect to?
- What product detail proves a larger benefit?
- What metaphor makes the idea instantly graspable?
- What two unrelated facts can be joined?

### Step 3 — Drop the Subject
When conscious effort stalls, stop forcing it. Put the problem aside and do something that stimulates the unconscious mind: walk, read, listen to music, sleep, or work on another task.

### Step 4 — Let the Idea Appear
After incubation, the idea often arrives unexpectedly. Capture it immediately before judging it.

### Step 5 — Shape and Test the Idea
Bring the idea into reality. Adapt it to practical conditions, refine it, expose it to criticism, and strengthen what works.

## Copywriting Applications

### Product/Customer Relationship Mining
Do not stop at surface facts. Study the product in relation to the customer until a distinctive relationship appears.

Example pattern:
- Generic claim: “This soap cleans skin.”
- Deeper relationship: “How this soap interacts with skin and hair over time.”
- Possible angle: education-based copy that makes the product feel uniquely suited to the buyer’s body or routine.

### Kaleidoscope Method
Each new fact is a piece of colored glass. Turn the kaleidoscope by pairing facts in new ways:
- Customer fear + product process
- Cultural trend + product benefit
- Scientific fact + headline metaphor
- Personal story + proof point
- Common objection + unexpected demonstration

### Headline Relationship Test
A small change in wording can change emotional meaning. Evaluate headline words as symbols, not just descriptors.

Questions:
- What emotion does this word carry?
- What belief does it activate?
- Does it connect the product to a deeper desire?
- Is there a more vivid, concrete, or unexpected word?

## Practical Worksheet

1. Define the copy problem in one sentence.
2. List 25 product facts.
3. List 25 customer facts in the customer’s own language.
4. List 10 competitor claims and clichés to avoid.
5. List 20 general-life references, analogies, or stories.
6. Combine facts into at least 20 “What if…” angles.
7. Step away for incubation.
8. Capture every idea that surfaces.
9. Choose the idea with the strongest mix of novelty, relevance, proof, and emotional pull.
10. Rewrite it into a headline, lead, offer angle, or campaign premise.
