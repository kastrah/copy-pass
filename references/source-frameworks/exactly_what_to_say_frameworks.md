# Exactly What to Say — Framework Summary

**Book by Phil M. Jones**

A practical guide to language patterns that lower resistance, clarify value, and help a reader or prospect take the next step. Use these as prompts, not scripts. The line must still sound natural in context.

## Core principle
Small wording changes can change how a person receives an idea. The goal is not manipulation; it is making the next useful decision easier to understand and easier to act on.

## Reusable language patterns

| Pattern | What it does | General example |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm not sure if it's for you, but..." | Lowers pressure and invites consideration | "I'm not sure if it's for you, but this checklist is useful before a launch." |
| "How open-minded would you be..." | Frames the ask as reasonable exploration | "How open-minded would you be to testing one workflow before changing the full system?" |
| "What do you know about..." | Reveals current beliefs before persuading | "What do you know about where users drop off after signup?" |
| "How would you feel if..." | Future-paces the emotional outcome | "How would you feel if every handoff had an owner before Monday?" |
| "Just imagine..." | Creates a mental picture of the after-state | "Just imagine opening the dashboard and seeing the next priority immediately." |
| "When would be a good time..." | Presupposes action and asks for timing | "When would be a good time to review the first draft?" |
| "I'm guessing you haven't got around to..." | Softens a follow-up without blame | "I'm guessing you haven't got around to reviewing the deck yet." |
| "Simple swaps" | Replaces weak phrasing with action-oriented phrasing | "Would you like to" → "When would you like to". |
| "You have three options..." | Simplifies decision-making | "You can keep the old flow, rebuild everything, or test one improvement first." |
| "There are two types of people..." | Creates identity contrast | "There are teams that wait for launch problems, and teams that rehearse the handoff first." |
| "The good news is..." | Pivots from problem to solution | "The good news is you do not need a full rebuild to fix the bottleneck." |
| "What happens next is..." | Removes uncertainty | "What happens next is we map the workflow, find the highest-friction step, and fix that first." |
| "Before you make your mind up..." | Buys attention before objection handling | "Before you make your mind up, look at the cost of the current workaround." |
| "If I can, will you..." | Pairs proof with commitment | "If I can show you the bottleneck in one page, will you review it with the team?" |
| "Enough?" | Tests threshold | "Would a 10-minute audit be enough to decide whether this is worth fixing?" |
| "Just one more thing..." | Reopens attention near the close | "Just one more thing: the biggest risk is usually the handoff nobody owns." |
| "A favor" | Makes a small ask feel personal and specific | "Could you do me a small favor and send the current process doc?" |
| "Would it help if..." | Offers support without sounding pushy | "Would it help if I turned this into a one-page checklist?" |

## Copywriting use cases

### Objection handling
Use before addressing resistance:
> "Before you make your mind up about changing the workflow, look at what the current process is costing every week."

### CTA softening
Use when the reader is not ready for a hard sell:
> "Would it help if you started with the audit checklist?"

### Process clarity
Use when uncertainty is the barrier:
> "What happens next is simple: choose one workflow, map the handoff, fix the highest-friction step."

### Future pacing
Use when the benefit needs to feel concrete:
> "Just imagine reviewing next week's launch and knowing every task already has an owner."

## Editing checklist

Before using a phrase:
1. Is this phrase reducing friction or just sounding clever?
2. Does it match the reader's belief level?
3. Is the next step clear?
4. Would a real person say this in this channel?
5. Can it be shorter?

## Pitfalls

- Do not stack too many magic phrases in one asset.
- Do not use the phrases to force commitment where trust has not been earned.
- Do not let the phrase become the idea. The underlying argument still has to work.
