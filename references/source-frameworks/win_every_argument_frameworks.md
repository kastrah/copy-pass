# How to Win Every Argument — Copywriting & Persuasion Framework Notes

**Source:** Madsen Pirie, practical guide to logical fallacies.

## Core usable idea
Persuasive copy wins by controlling relevance, evidence, framing, and burden of proof. Fallacies are useful to study not as permission to deceive, but as a checklist for making arguments stronger, spotting weak claims, and understanding how audiences are often moved by shortcuts.

## Ethical stance for marketing use
Use this source defensively and structurally:
- Avoid arguments that are logically weak or misleading.
- Recognize fallacies prospects may use in objections.
- Strengthen proof where readers are likely to distrust claims.
- Use framing devices ethically: contrast, analogy, authority, consequence, and social proof should clarify—not fabricate.

## Argument-strength checklist
Before publishing persuasive copy, test:
- Is the main claim clear?
- Does the evidence actually support the claim?
- Are terms used consistently?
- Are comparisons fair and relevant?
- Are exceptions acknowledged when they matter?
- Is causation proven, or merely implied?
- Is authority relevant and credible?
- Is the CTA based on a real reason, not a false dilemma?

## Framing and relevance fallacies
### Red herring
A distracting point diverts attention from the real issue.

Copy lesson:
- Do not overfill pages with irrelevant benefits.
- Keep proof tied to the buyer’s decision.
- In objection handling, return to the central question: “Will this help me solve my problem?”

### Ignoratio elenchi / irrelevant conclusion
The argument proves something, but not the thing that needs proving.

Copy lesson:
- Awards, features, and credentials are only useful if they support the buyer’s desired outcome.
- Every section should answer a real buying question.

### Trivial objections
Minor points are used to avoid the central argument.

Copy lesson:
- Preempt small objections in FAQ or footnotes so they do not derail the sale.
- Separate “deal-breaker” objections from “detail” objections.

## Comparison and analogy tools
### Abusive analogy
An analogy is used to make something look bad by association.

Ethical use:
- Use analogy to simplify, not smear.
- Compare the problem to a familiar pattern when it helps understanding.
- Avoid cheap competitor attacks that undermine trust.

### Analogical fallacy
The comparison breaks because the two things differ in important ways.

Copy lesson:
- If using analogy, make the relevant similarity explicit.
- Do not let a clever metaphor carry a claim that needs proof.

### Composition and division
Assuming what is true of a part is true of the whole, or vice versa.

Copy lesson:
- Do not overclaim from one feature to total product superiority.
- Do not assume one customer result proves universal outcomes.
- Use qualified language where evidence is limited.

## Cause-and-effect fallacies
### Post hoc ergo propter hoc
Assuming that because B followed A, A caused B.

Copy lesson:
- Be careful with case studies and before/after claims.
- Show mechanism, context, and supporting evidence.
- Use “associated with,” “helped,” or “contributed to” when causation is not fully proven.

### Cum hoc ergo propter hoc
Assuming correlation proves causation.

Copy lesson:
- Do not imply that a metric improved solely because of the product unless evidence supports it.
- Add context: baseline, timeframe, other variables, and method.

### Ex-post-facto statistics
Selecting data after the fact to support a story.

Copy lesson:
- Avoid cherry-picked metrics.
- Define success metrics before campaigns where possible.
- In public proof, show the most decision-relevant data, not only the most flattering data.

## Authority and credibility fallacies
### Argumentum ad verecundiam / appeal to authority
Using authority as proof.

Ethical use:
- Authority helps when the expert is relevant to the claim.
- Pair authority with evidence, demonstration, or explanation.
- Avoid borrowed prestige from irrelevant celebrities or vague “experts say” claims.

### Blinding with science
Technical complexity is used to intimidate rather than clarify.

Copy lesson:
- Technical proof should be translated into buyer meaning.
- Use plain-language explanations after technical claims.
- If jargon does not increase trust or precision, cut it.

### Every schoolboy knows
Treating a claim as obvious to avoid proving it.

Copy lesson:
- Replace “everyone knows” assumptions with evidence or shared observation.
- If a belief is common, still explain why it matters.

## Emotion and pressure fallacies
### Emotional appeals
Emotion is used in place of evidence.

Copy lesson:
- Emotion is powerful, but it should point toward a truthful claim.
- Pair fear, aspiration, pride, relief, or belonging with proof and a clear next step.

### Argumentum ad baculum / appeal to force
A threat substitutes for a reason.

Copy lesson:
- Urgency must be real.
- Do not manufacture consequences.
- Use genuine stakes: missed deadline, limited capacity, expiring bonus, known risk, opportunity cost.

### Argumentum ad misericordiam / appeal to pity
Pity is used to win agreement.

Copy lesson:
- Founder stories and mission claims should support the buyer’s decision, not guilt them into action.

### Appeal to fear
Not always listed as a single fallacy in the same way, but fear-based reasoning can become manipulative when disproportionate.

Ethical formula:
**Real risk + credible explanation + proportional language + practical solution**

## Choice architecture fallacies
### Bifurcation / false dilemma / bogus dilemma
Presenting two choices when more exist.

Copy lesson:
- Binary framing is persuasive, but should not be false.
- Use fair contrasts: “If your priority is X, choose A; if Y, choose B.”
- Avoid “buy now or fail” unless the stakes are genuinely that binary.

### Damning the alternatives
Rejecting all other options without fair evaluation.

Copy lesson:
- Competitive comparison should be specific and evidence-based.
- “Why this instead of alternatives” is stronger than vague dismissal.

### Unobtainable perfection
Rejecting an option because it is not perfect.

Copy use:
- Useful for handling hesitation: “The question is not whether the solution is perfect; it is whether it is meaningfully better than the current state.”

## Language and ambiguity fallacies
### Equivocation
A word changes meaning inside the argument.

Copy lesson:
- Define key terms: fast, premium, guaranteed, natural, proven, simple, automated, full-service.
- Keep terminology consistent across page, ad, and offer.

### Amphiboly
Grammar creates ambiguity.

Copy lesson:
- Read claims for possible misinterpretation.
- Avoid stacked modifiers and unclear pronouns.

### Loaded words
Language smuggles judgment into description.

Ethical use:
- Loaded words can create positioning, but should match reality.
- Use strong language for real differences, not to disguise weak proof.

### False precision
Exact numbers imply certainty that may not exist.

Copy lesson:
- Use precise numbers when real.
- Use ranges or qualified estimates when precision is impossible.
- Do not overstate confidence with fake specificity.

## Social proof and popularity fallacies
### Argumentum ad populum
A claim is treated as true because many people believe it.

Copy lesson:
- Popularity can reduce perceived risk, but it does not prove quality.
- Pair social proof with outcome proof.

### Argumentum ad numeram
Numbers of believers/supporters are used as proof.

Copy lesson:
- “Thousands use it” is useful, but incomplete.
- Add: who uses it, why they use it, what result or benefit they get.

### Argumentum ad antiquitatem / novelty
Oldness or newness is treated as proof of superiority.

Copy lesson:
- “Established” should imply reliability.
- “New” should imply improvement.
- Neither should stand alone as the reason to buy.

## Burden of proof and objection handling
### Shifting the burden of proof
Demanding the skeptic disprove the claim.

Copy lesson:
- The seller carries the burden for meaningful claims.
- Provide proof before demanding trust.

### Argumentum ad ignorantiam
A claim is treated as true because it has not been disproven.

Copy lesson:
- Avoid “no one has proven otherwise” reasoning.
- Use positive evidence.

### Special pleading
Making exceptions for your claim without justification.

Copy lesson:
- If the offer is different, explain the mechanism.
- “We’re different” is not proof.

## Straw man and competitor positioning
### Straw man
Misrepresenting the opposing position to defeat it easily.

Copy lesson:
- Do not caricature competitors or customer objections.
- Strong copy states the alternative fairly, then shows why the offer is better for a specific use case.

Better formula:
**Fair alternative → limitation in context → stronger fit → proof**

## Repetition and persistence
### Argumentum ad nauseam
Repeating a claim until it feels true.

Ethical application:
- Repeat core positioning consistently across touchpoints.
- Do not repeat unsupported claims; repeat proved, useful memory structures.
- Use repetition for brand memory, not deception.

## Fallacy-to-copy audit
Use this when reviewing sales pages, ads, emails, and scripts:
- **Claim:** What are we asking the reader to believe?
- **Evidence:** What proves it?
- **Relevance:** Does the evidence answer the buying question?
- **Causation:** Are we proving cause or only showing sequence/correlation?
- **Language:** Are key terms clear and consistent?
- **Comparison:** Is the alternative represented fairly?
- **Emotion:** Is emotional pressure proportionate and truthful?
- **Choice:** Are options framed honestly?
- **Authority:** Is the cited authority relevant?
- **CTA:** Does the action follow logically from the argument?

## Practical application notes
- Use fallacies as a quality-control checklist.
- Build stronger copy by removing logical gaps before adding emotional force.
- Preempt objections by identifying where readers may see weak reasoning.
- Let clarity, proof, and relevance do most of the persuasion.
- Use rhetorical devices to sharpen true claims, not hide unsupported ones.
