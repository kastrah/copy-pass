# Engagement Is the New Cocaine — Copywriting & Persuasion Framework Notes

**Source:** *Engagement Is the New Cocaine*.

## Core usable idea
Social growth comes from writing that earns **hard engagement**: shares, quote-shares, follows, replies with substance, and clicks. Impressions and likes are weaker signals. The copy must deliver enough value, emotion, identity, or tension that people feel compelled to pass it on.

## The engagement hierarchy
Prioritize actions in this order:
1. **Retweet/share:** expands distribution.
2. **Quote/share with commentary:** adds social proof and context.
3. **Follow/subscribe:** converts attention into audience.
4. **Click:** transfers attention to the next asset.
5. **Reply:** useful when it deepens conversation or reach.
6. **Like:** weakest meaningful signal.

Copy implication: optimize for “I need other people to see this,” not merely “I agree.”

## Value rule
Every post should do at least one of four things:
- **Solve a problem directly.**
- **Educate people about a solution.**
- **Persuade them to feel differently about a problem.**
- **Entertain them enough to briefly escape the problem.**

If a post does none of these, it is noise.

## Hard-engagement checklist
Before publishing, ask:
- Is the point clear in one read?
- Does it contain a useful, emotional, contrarian, or identity-based reason to share?
- Does it speak to a problem the audience already recognizes?
- Does it create tension, surprise, relief, or recognition?
- Is there one idea, not three?
- Could the first line stand alone as a hook?
- Are weak qualifiers removed?
- Is the language simple enough to create an immediate reaction?

## Negativity for attention
People notice threats, losses, mistakes, enemies, constraints, and unfairness faster than neutral positives. Use negativity to create attention, then pair it with value.

### Forms of useful negativity
- **Word-choice negativity:** use sharp, simple negative words to create emotional force.
- **Explicit negativity:** name what is broken, dangerous, wasteful, false, or overrated.
- **Implicit negativity:** imply a hidden cost, trap, or force working against the reader.
- **Relatable negativity:** articulate a frustration the audience privately feels.

Formula:
**Problem/irritant + plain negative language + useful reframe or action = engaging post**

Ethical guardrail: attack the problem, pattern, behavior, or belief more than a vulnerable person or group.

## Absolutes framework
Absolute statements drive engagement because they are easy to agree with, reject, quote, or debate. Nuance may be more accurate, but absolutes travel faster.

Examples of absolute structures:
- “The fastest way to lose trust is…”
- “Nobody remembers…”
- “Every strong offer needs…”
- “The worst advice in [category] is…”
- “If you can’t explain [thing], you don’t understand it.”

Use absolutes for:
- Lessons learned through experience.
- Strong category opinions.
- Common mistakes.
- Identity statements.
- Rules of thumb.

Avoid absolutes when:
- The claim is factually fragile.
- The topic requires legal, medical, financial, or safety precision.
- The post would create unnecessary reputational risk.

## Polarization without trolling
Engagement rises when the audience can locate themselves in relation to the claim.

Useful polarity types:
- Old way vs new way.
- Amateur vs professional.
- Noise vs signal.
- Short-term hack vs durable skill.
- Vanity metric vs meaningful metric.
- Comfortable belief vs uncomfortable truth.

Template:
**“Most people think [common belief]. But [contrary truth]. The real issue is [deeper principle].”**

## Parallelism
Parallelism makes short copy memorable by repeating grammatical structure.

Uses:
- Lists.
- Contrasts.
- Mini-manifestos.
- Thread summaries.
- Landing page bullets.

Patterns:
- “More [A], less [B].”
- “Not because [X]. Because [Y].”
- “You don’t need [A]. You need [B].”
- “It saves [thing]. It reduces [thing]. It creates [thing].”

Checklist:
- Keep repeated clauses similar in length.
- Put the strongest line last.
- Use concrete nouns and verbs.
- Cut filler between repeated beats.

## Anaphora
Anaphora repeats the opening of successive lines to create rhythm and emphasis.

Pattern:
- “You can…”
- “You can…”
- “You can…”
- “But until you…”

Best uses:
- Building emotional momentum.
- Stacking benefits.
- Naming frustrations.
- Creating manifesto-style copy.
- Ending with a turn or CTA.

## Common-experience framework
The fastest way to feel original is to describe a common experience with unusual clarity.

Prompts:
- What does the audience complain about privately?
- What have they felt but not articulated?
- What daily friction do they treat as normal?
- What embarrassing mistake can be reframed as a shared pattern?
- What “everyone knows” but few say plainly?

Formula:
**Common moment + precise observation + emotional label + takeaway**

## Simplicity rules for short-form copy
- One post = one idea.
- Use short sentences.
- Prefer strong nouns and verbs to adjectives.
- Remove throat-clearing.
- Lead with the tension, not the background.
- Write for scanning first, appreciation second.
- Make the ending snap: lesson, twist, CTA, or memorable line.

## Social post formulas
### 1. Negative truth + reframe
“[Common behavior] is why [bad result]. The fix is not [obvious fix]. It is [better principle].”

### 2. Absolute lesson
“Every [audience] eventually learns this: [lesson].”

### 3. Contrast stack
“Bad [thing]: [description]. Good [thing]: [description]. Great [thing]: [description].”

### 4. Relatable frustration
“Nothing slows [audience] down like [specific friction]. Not [surface issue]. [Deeper issue].”

### 5. Shareable rule
“If [condition], don’t [common action]. Do [better action].”

### 6. Identity trigger
“Amateurs [behavior]. Professionals [behavior].”

## Application notes beyond Twitter/X
- **Email subject lines:** use absolutes, negative stakes, and common frustrations.
- **Ad hooks:** lead with the painful pattern before the product.
- **Landing page bullets:** use parallelism for benefit stacks.
- **Thought leadership:** use polarized but defensible claims.
- **Threads/carousels:** each section should deliver one clear value unit.

## Final publishing checklist
- Does it add value?
- Does it invite hard engagement?
- Is the language direct and simple?
- Is there a clear emotional charge?
- Is the claim defensible?
- Is the strongest line first or last?
- Would the intended audience share this to signal taste, intelligence, identity, or usefulness?
