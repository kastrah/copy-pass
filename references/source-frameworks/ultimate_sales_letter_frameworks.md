# The Ultimate Sales Letter — Public Copywriting Frameworks

**Source:** Dan S. Kennedy, *The Ultimate Sales Letter*.

Concise reusable frameworks for direct-mail letters, emails, landing pages, ads, and sales pages.

## Core Thesis

A sales letter is salesmanship in print. It must move the reader through **AIDA**:

1. **Attention** — get noticed.
2. **Interest** — become relevant and valuable.
3. **Desire** — make the outcome emotionally compelling.
4. **Action** — make response immediate and easy.

## Kennedy's Sales Letter System — Condensed

### Before writing
1. Get into the customer.
2. Get into the offer.
3. Identify flaws and damaging admissions.
4. Plan delivery and openability.

### Drafting
5. Get the letter looked at.
6. Get it read with a strong headline/lead.
7. Beat price resistance.
8. Use proven copy tactics.
9. Write the first draft.
10. Rewrite for strategy.
11. Rewrite for style.
12. Answer objections.
13. Spark immediate action.
14. Use a strong P.S.
15. Check against checklists.

### Production and improvement
16. Add graphic enhancement.
17. Rewrite for passion and edit for clarity.
18. Compare to examples.
19. Pretest.
20. Bring the letter to life.
21. Change/test graphic enhancements.
22. Edit again.
23. Mail a mockup.
24. Cool off before final approval.
25. Get second opinions.
26. Final review.
27. Produce.
28. Mail/test/track.

## 10 Smart Market Diagnosis Questions

Use before writing:

1. What keeps them awake at night?
2. What are they afraid of?
3. What are they angry about, and who are they angry at?
4. What are their top three daily frustrations?
5. What trends are affecting their business or life?
6. What do they secretly and ardently desire most?
7. Do they have a built-in decision-making bias? Example: analytical, skeptical, status-driven, convenience-driven.
8. Do they have their own language, jargon, or insider phrases?
9. Who else is selling something similar to them, and how?
10. Who else has tried selling them something similar, and how did that effort fail?

## Get Into the Customer

Do not write to demographics alone. Visualize the recipient's day:
- Where are they when they receive the message?
- What else are they thinking about?
- What pressures are on them?
- What do they complain about?
- What do they secretly wish for?
- What language do they use to describe the problem?

The more the message sounds like it understands the reader's life, the less it feels like an unwanted interruption.

## Get Into the Offer

An offer is more than the product. Define:
- Core product/service.
- Main result promised.
- Bonuses or added value.
- Guarantee/risk reversal.
- Payment terms.
- Deadline or scarcity.
- Response method.
- Emotional reason to act.

Strong offer formula:
> For [specific audience], get [specific result] with [mechanism], plus [bonus/proof], protected by [guarantee], if you respond by [deadline].

## Damaging Admission Framework

Address obvious flaws before the reader does.

Use when:
- The price is high.
- The method is unusual.
- The product is not for everyone.
- There is a limitation, delay, inconvenience, or requirement.

Structure:
1. Admit the flaw plainly.
2. Explain why it exists.
3. Reframe it as a sign of quality, honesty, selectivity, or fit.
4. Show why the benefit still outweighs the drawback.

Example pattern:
> “This is not the cheapest option. It is built for people who value [outcome] more than [cheap alternative]. Here is why that difference matters...”

## From Pest to Welcome Guest

Nobody waits eagerly for a sales message. Earn attention by immediately saying something the reader recognizes as important, valuable, urgent, or beneficial.

Avoid empty gimmicks. Lead with relevance.

Checklist:
- Does the first screen/page promise a reader-valued result?
- Is the lead about the reader, not the company?
- Is there urgency or significance?
- Is the headline specific enough to stop the right person?

## Headline and Johnson Box Framework

Sales letters can use a headline above or below the salutation. A Johnson box highlights the main promise before the body copy.

Headline's job:
- Stop the reader.
- Melt resistance.
- Promise value.
- Pull the reader into the first paragraph.

## Fill-in-the-Blank Headline Formulas

Reusable public headline structures:

- **They didn't think I could ___, but I did.**
  - Underdog story; overcoming doubt.

- **Who else wants ___?**
  - Implies others already desire or possess the benefit.

- **How ___ made me ___.**
  - Personal transformation story.

- **Are you ___?**
  - Question that challenges, qualifies, or provokes curiosity.

- **How I ___.**
  - First-person proof/story.

- **How to ___.**
  - Direct practical benefit.

- **For [specific audience]: how to ___.**
  - Adds a flag that calls out the right reader.

- **If you are ___, you can ___.**
  - Qualification plus promise.

- **Secrets of ___.**
  - Curiosity and insider knowledge.

- **Thousands now ___ even though they ___.**
  - Social proof plus overcome-objection angle.

- **Warning: ___.**
  - Strong for problem/solution copy.

## First Paragraph as Extended Headline

The headline sells the first paragraph. The first paragraph sells the rest of the letter.

Use the opening to:
- Restate the key promise in plain language.
- Signal urgency or importance.
- Confirm the message is for this exact reader.
- Create enough curiosity to continue.

## Price Resistance Framework

Beat the “price bugaboo” by reframing cost.

Methods:
- Compare price to the value of the result.
- Compare price to the cost of the problem.
- Break price into smaller units: per day, per use, per lead, per saved hour.
- Show return on investment.
- Offer payment terms.
- Add bonuses.
- Use guarantees and trials.
- Show that inaction is more expensive.

## Internal Repetition Framework

Repeat the central promise in multiple forms rather than relying on one statement.

Deliver the same core message through:
1. Straight statement.
2. Example.
3. Story or slice of life.
4. Testimonial.
5. Quote from customer/expert/spokesperson.
6. Numbered summary.

Goal: the reader absorbs the point without feeling the copy is merely repeating itself.

## Yes-Sequence Framework

Build agreement before asking for action.

Use:
- Questions the reader can easily answer “yes” to.
- Known facts they already accept.
- Frustrations they recognize.
- Step-by-step logic that makes the offer feel like the natural conclusion.

Example structure:
> “Have you noticed [problem]? Have you tried [common failed solution]? Would it help if [desired result]? Then...”

## Readability and Style Checklist

Make the letter reader-friendly:
- Short sentences.
- Short paragraphs.
- Everyday language.
- Subheads and bullets.
- Specific examples.
- Conversational tone.
- Visual breaks.
- No corporate throat-clearing.
- No boring stretches.

Length is not the main problem; boredom is.

## Entertaining Without Being Gimmicky

Kennedy distinguishes “interesting” from merely funny.

Use:
- Light story.
- Personal voice.
- Vivid situation.
- Surprise.
- Specific details.
- Strong point of view.

Avoid humor that distracts from the sale or depends on the reader sharing the joke.

## Sensory and Emotional Copy

People buy emotionally and justify logically.

Add passion by describing:
- What the problem feels like.
- What relief feels like.
- What the result lets the reader do.
- What worry, frustration, pride, freedom, safety, or status is involved.

If the product is “boring,” dramatize the emotional benefit: fewer worries, saved time, avoided embarrassment, confidence, control, freedom.

## Objection-Answering Framework

List every reason the reader might not act, then answer in the copy.

Common objections:
- Too expensive.
- Too risky.
- Too complicated.
- Not for me.
- I don't believe it.
- I don't need it now.
- I need to ask someone.
- I've tried something similar and failed.

Answer with proof, examples, guarantee, comparison, deadline, or simplified next step.

## Immediate Action Framework

A sales letter must not create “someday” interest. It must create now action.

Use:
- Deadline.
- Limited quantity or capacity.
- Bonus for fast response.
- Price increase date.
- Event date.
- Seasonal/timing reason.
- Cost of delay.
- Simple response mechanism.

## P.S. Framework

The P.S. is often read early and late. Use it to reinforce:
- Main promise.
- Deadline/urgency.
- Guarantee.
- Bonus.
- Simple next step.

Example pattern:
> “P.S. Remember, you can try [offer] risk-free for [period]. Respond by [date] and you also receive [bonus]. Use [method] now.”

## Graphic Enhancement Guidelines

Design should increase readership, not decorate for its own sake.

Useful devices:
- Johnson box.
- Subheads.
- Bullets and numbered lists.
- Callouts.
- Underlining/highlighting sparingly.
- Handwritten-style notes where appropriate.
- Photos, charts, proof visuals.
- Order form or response device that looks easy to use.

## Sequence Principle

One letter can work, but a sequence often works better.

Sequence uses:
- First contact: introduce problem and promise.
- Follow-up: add proof, story, or case study.
- Next contact: answer objections.
- Deadline contact: intensify urgency.
- Final contact: recap offer and last chance.

Do not expect one exposure to carry all persuasion when the decision is complex or expensive.

## Final Review Checklist

Before release:
- Is the audience sharply defined?
- Does the headline stop the right reader?
- Does the first paragraph extend the headline?
- Is the offer clear and compelling?
- Is the central promise repeated in varied ways?
- Are objections answered?
- Is price reframed against value/cost of inaction?
- Is there proof?
- Is there a guarantee or risk reducer?
- Is there a reason to act immediately?
- Is the call to action unmistakable?
- Is the copy easy to read visually?
- Is it passionate, specific, and non-boring?
- Has it been pretested or reviewed by someone outside the writing process?

## Adaptable Mini-Formula

**Diagnose the market → craft a strong offer → admit and reframe flaws → grab attention → build interest with relevance → create desire with proof and emotion → answer objections → add urgency → close with an easy action → follow up in sequence.**
