# Copywriting 101 — Framework Notes

**Source:** Brian Clark / Copyblogger, *Copywriting 101: How to Craft Compelling Copy*.
**Use for:** foundational direct-response structure, headline writing, benefits extraction, offer framing, and conversion-page diagnostics.

## Core premise

Copywriting is strategic communication designed to get a reader to take a specific action. The craft is less about clever phrasing and more about sequencing attention, desire, belief, and action.

## 1. The “next sentence” rule

**Principle:** Every element of copy has one job: get the next element consumed.

- Headline → get the first sentence read.
- First sentence → get the second sentence read.
- Opening → create enough relevance to continue.
- Body → build desire and belief.
- Offer → make the exchange feel valuable and low-risk.
- CTA → convert attention into action.

**Application checklist**

- Does the headline create a clear reason to start reading?
- Is the first sentence short, easy, and connected to the headline?
- Does each paragraph naturally raise or answer the next question?
- Are subheads functioning as re-entry points for scanners?
- Does every section either increase desire, reduce doubt, prove a claim, or move toward action?

## 2. Clarity beats cleverness

**Principle:** Good copy is clear, concise, and simple. Complexity increases friction.

**Rules of thumb**

- Prefer plain words over impressive words.
- Use short sentences where momentum matters.
- Break grammar rules only when doing so improves rhythm or comprehension.
- Avoid insider jargon unless the audience uses it and expects it.
- Make the value proposition understandable without rereading.

**Diagnostic questions**

- Would a busy reader understand the promise in five seconds?
- Can the main idea be repeated by someone after one read?
- Are any sentences performing intellectual display instead of persuasion?

## 3. Headline priority: the 50/50 and 80/20 rules

**Principle:** Headlines deserve disproportionate effort because most readers decide whether to continue based on them.

- **50/50 rule:** Spend a large share of writing time on the headline and lead.
- **80/20 rule:** Many people read the headline; far fewer read the body. A weak headline wastes strong copy.

**Headline job description**

A headline should promise a benefit, arouse curiosity, identify the right audience, or present a compelling reason to read now.

## 4. Headline categories that work

Use these as starting points, not rigid templates.

- **Direct promise:** State the result plainly.
- **How-to:** Promise practical instruction.
- **Reason-why:** Give numbered or structured reasons.
- **Question:** Surface a problem or desired outcome the reader already has.
- **Command:** Tell the reader to take a beneficial action.
- **News/announcement:** Present something new, improved, released, discovered, or changed.
- **Testimonial/social proof:** Let proof or user experience carry the claim.
- **Problem/mistake:** Name a costly error or hidden obstacle.

## 5. The Four U’s headline test

Headlines, subheads, and bullets should be evaluated against four criteria:

- **Useful:** Does it promise something the reader values?
- **Urgent:** Is there a reason to pay attention now?
- **Unique:** Does it avoid sounding interchangeable?
- **Ultra-specific:** Does it make the promise concrete?

**Scoring method**

Rate each U from 1–4. Strengthen the lowest score first. A headline does not need to max every category, but it should not be vague, generic, or purely clever.

## 6. Benefit extraction: feature → advantage → true benefit → emotional root

**Principle:** Features explain what something is or does. Benefits explain why the reader should care.

**Extraction ladder**

1. **Feature:** What does the product/service/content include?
2. **Functional advantage:** What does that feature enable?
3. **True benefit:** What desirable outcome does the reader get?
4. **Emotional root:** What feeling, identity, relief, status, safety, freedom, or confidence does that outcome create?

**Forehead-slap test**

A true benefit should make the reader think, “Yes, that’s exactly what I want.” If the statement merely describes the product, it is probably still a feature or fake benefit.

## 7. Sell with benefits, support with features

**Principle:** Benefits create desire; features support belief.

Use benefits to answer:

- Why should I care?
- What changes for me?
- What pain goes away?
- What opportunity becomes possible?

Use features to answer:

- How does it work?
- Why should I believe it can deliver?
- What exactly am I getting?
- Is this suitable for my situation?

**Application note:** Technical or B2B audiences may require more feature detail, but the feature still needs a business or personal payoff.

## 8. Persuasive copy structure

A general-purpose sequence:

1. **Attention:** headline, opening hook, relevant problem or promise.
2. **Connection:** show you understand the reader’s situation.
3. **Problem/desire:** clarify what is at stake.
4. **Promise:** introduce the desired outcome.
5. **Proof:** evidence, mechanism, specifics, credibility, demonstration.
6. **Offer:** package the value into a clear exchange.
7. **Risk reversal:** guarantee, trial, reassurance, objection handling.
8. **Action:** direct CTA with clear next step.

## 9. The irresistible offer as a story

**Principle:** Offers are more persuasive when framed as a desirable story, not just a transaction.

An effective offer narrative answers:

- What problem or desire brings the reader here?
- What better future is being offered?
- Why is this solution credible?
- Why is the exchange fair or advantageous?
- What happens immediately after the reader acts?

**Offer components**

- Clear outcome.
- Clear deliverables.
- Clear price or required commitment.
- Clear reason to act.
- Clear reduction of risk.
- Clear next step.

## 10. Risk reversal and guarantees

**Principle:** The buyer’s perceived risk often blocks action more than lack of interest.

Ways to reduce risk:

- Money-back guarantee.
- Free trial or sample.
- Cancel-anytime structure.
- Proof before payment.
- Demonstration or preview.
- Clear expectation-setting.
- Keep-some-value return premium, where appropriate.

**Checklist**

- What could make the reader hesitate?
- Can that risk be removed, reduced, or reframed?
- Is the guarantee simple to understand?
- Does the guarantee strengthen confidence rather than sound desperate?

## 11. Copy as an invitation/contract

**Principle:** Persuasion works when the reader sees a fair exchange: “If I do this, I get that.”

Make the implied contract explicit:

- If you subscribe, you receive X.
- If you book a call, you will learn Y.
- If you buy, you get Z outcome or asset.
- If it is not right, here is what happens.

Vague next steps create anxiety. Specific next steps create momentum.

## 12. Practical editing pass

Run every draft through these passes:

1. **Promise pass:** Is the main benefit obvious?
2. **Reader pass:** Is the copy about the reader more than the seller?
3. **Specificity pass:** Replace vague claims with concrete outcomes, mechanisms, numbers, or conditions.
4. **Proof pass:** Add evidence where belief may fail.
5. **Friction pass:** Remove confusing language and unnecessary steps.
6. **Momentum pass:** Strengthen transitions so each line pulls into the next.
7. **CTA pass:** Make the action unmistakable.

## Best uses in Copy Pass

- Diagnose weak headlines with the Four U’s.
- Convert feature-heavy copy into benefit-led messaging.
- Improve landing pages by checking sentence-to-sentence momentum.
- Strengthen offers by clarifying the exchange, proof, and risk reversal.
- Simplify copy that is accurate but not persuasive.
