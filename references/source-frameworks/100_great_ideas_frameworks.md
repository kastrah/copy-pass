# 100 Great Copywriting Ideas — Practical Framework Extraction

**Source:** Andy Maslen, *100 Great Copywriting Ideas* (Marshall Cavendish, 2009).
**Use case:** Copy Pass reference notes for writing, reviewing, and improving direct-response copy.  
**Core thesis:** Copy is a substitute for a sales conversation. Its job is not to sound clever; its job is to identify the right reader, speak in their language, make a relevant promise, prove it, handle resistance, and ask for action.

This is not a mechanical list of all 100 ideas. It compresses recurring lessons into practical frameworks, checklists, and review questions.

---

## 1. The Copy Pass Master Sequence

Use this sequence when reviewing any page, email, ad, landing page, brochure, or direct-mail piece.

1. **Reader:** Who exactly is this for?
2. **Motivation:** What do they already want, fear, envy, crave, or aspire to?
3. **Problem:** What pain, friction, risk, cost, or missed opportunity are they living with?
4. **Promise:** What specific improvement are we offering?
5. **Offer:** What do they get, under what terms, and why now?
6. **Proof:** Why should they believe us?
7. **Objections:** What would make them hesitate?
8. **Clarity:** Can they understand it quickly without working?
9. **Action:** What should they do next?
10. **Test:** What variable could most improve response?

If any one of these is vague, the copy is probably underperforming.

## 2. Research: Know the Person Before Writing the Pitch

Maslen repeatedly returns to the idea that copy begins before writing. The writer must know the reader as a person, not as an abstract “target market.”

### Research objectives

Find the reader’s:

- **Self-interest:** money, time, status, safety, ease, health, recognition, belonging, control, enjoyment.
- **Pain points:** what annoys, delays, worries, embarrasses, costs, or frustrates them.
- **Aspirations:** who they want to become, what they want to own, how they want to be seen.
- **Fears:** loss, wasted money, looking foolish, complexity, hidden costs, risk, change.
- **Language:** exact words customers use to describe the problem and desired outcome.
- **Buying context:** who decides, who influences, what alternatives are being compared.
- **Barriers:** price, trust, time, effort, approval, skepticism, confusion.

### Practical research checklist

Before drafting, answer:

- Who is the one reader I am writing to?
- What would make them stop and say, “That’s for me”?
- What are they already motivated by?
- What do they believe now that might be wrong or incomplete?
- What do they need to know before they can say yes?
- What proof would they trust most?
- What words would they use if describing this to a friend?

### Useful methods

- Read customer reviews, complaints, comments, support tickets, sales-call notes, and FAQs.
- Interview sales/support teams for common objections and recurring phrases.
- Build a simple questionnaire when assumptions are weak.
- Keep a “magpie file” of strong headlines, offers, structures, CTAs, proof devices, and formats.
- Study good copy for structure, not just wording.

### Copy Pass diagnostic

If the copy says “we,” “our,” or “the company” more often than “you,” “your,” or reader-specific language, it may be brand-centered rather than customer-centered.

## 3. Headlines: Stop the Right Reader

A headline does not need to close the sale. It needs to earn the next line by calling out a strong motivation, outcome, problem, objection, or curiosity gap.

### Headline jobs

A strong headline usually does at least one of these:

- Names the reader or situation.
- Promises a desirable result.
- Solves or relieves a specific pain.
- Handles a likely objection.
- Creates curiosity without becoming obscure.
- Uses specificity: numbers, timeframes, constraints, or concrete nouns.
- Signals urgency, novelty, or immediacy.

### Headline frameworks

- **Self-interest:** “For people who want [desired outcome].”
- **How-to:** “How to [achieve result] without [major objection].”
- **Objection-handling:** “[Benefit] without [feared cost/risk/sacrifice].”
- **Question:** “What would happen if you could [desired change]?”
- **Now/new:** “Now you can [result] with/without [constraint].”
- **Specific number:** “[Number] ways to [outcome] in [time/context].”
- **Corrective:** “The mistake that keeps [audience] from [result].”
- **True/false curiosity:** “True or false: [surprising claim]?”

### Headline review questions

- Does this headline speak to the reader’s self-interest?
- Is the main benefit obvious?
- Is there a concrete reason to keep reading?
- Could adding a number, timeframe, audience, or objection make it stronger?
- Is it clever at the expense of clarity?
- Would the reader know this is for them in three seconds?

## 4. Openings: Start Where the Reader Already Is

The opening should not warm up for the writer’s benefit. It should quickly connect with the reader’s situation and move toward the sale.

### Effective opening patterns

- **Direct problem:** “If you’re struggling with [problem], here’s why it happens.”
- **Reader desire:** “You want [result], but [obstacle] keeps getting in the way.”
- **Story:** Open with a concrete situation that mirrors the reader’s experience.
- **Question:** Ask something that gets the reader nodding.
- **Imagination:** Invite the reader to picture the better future.
- **Contrarian correction:** Challenge an assumption the reader may hold.
- **Fast offer:** Put the product, deal, or result in front of them early.

### Opening rules

- Cut throat-clearing, history lessons, corporate introductions, and “we are delighted to announce.”
- Do not hide the sell for too long.
- Make the first paragraph easy to enter: short, concrete, reader-focused.
- Build an agreement chain: get the reader nodding before asking for belief or action.
- Use “you” naturally, but do not fake intimacy.

### Copy Pass diagnostic

If the first paragraph could be deleted without weakening the copy, delete or rewrite it.

## 5. Offers: Give People a Reason to Act

Maslen emphasizes that both consumers and business buyers respond to offers. Sophistication does not eliminate human attraction to value, savings, exclusivity, convenience, or bonuses.

### Offer elements

A complete offer clarifies:

- **What they get:** product, service, bonus, content, consultation, trial, sample, guarantee.
- **What it costs:** money, time, effort, commitment, data, risk.
- **Why it is valuable:** savings, scarcity, convenience, access, status, speed, reduced risk.
- **Why now:** deadline, quantity limit, timely relevance, launch window, expiring bonus.
- **What to do:** the exact next step.

### Offer checklist

- Is the offer visible early, not buried at the end?
- Is it repeated in varied language across the copy?
- Is the value proportional to the action requested?
- Is the limitation believable: time, quantity, eligibility, or availability?
- Are savings expressed in the most persuasive way: percentage, cash amount, time saved, risk avoided?
- Is there a reason to respond now rather than later?

### Offer formulas

- **Discount:** “Save [amount/%] when you [action] by [deadline].”
- **Bonus:** “Get [bonus] when you [primary action].”
- **Risk reversal:** “Try [offer] for [period] with [guarantee/low-risk exit].”
- **Access:** “Reserve your place / get early access / join the first [number].”
- **Bundled value:** “[Core product] plus [supporting assets] for [price/condition].”

## 6. Benefits: Translate Features Into Human Outcomes

A recurring framework is FAB: **Features, Advantages, Benefits**. Features describe what it is. Advantages explain what it does. Benefits explain why the reader should care.

### FAB translation

For every feature, ask:

1. **Feature:** What is it?
2. **Advantage:** What does it enable?
3. **Benefit:** What desirable change does the reader experience?
4. **Emotional payoff:** How does that make them feel or look?

Example structure:

- Feature: “Automated reporting.”
- Advantage: “Reports are generated without manual spreadsheet work.”
- Benefit: “You save time and reduce errors.”
- Emotional payoff: “You feel in control before the Monday meeting.”

### Benefit types to look for

- Make money.
- Save money.
- Save time.
- Reduce effort.
- Reduce risk.
- Increase status.
- Avoid embarrassment.
- Gain confidence.
- Enjoy pleasure or comfort.
- Protect family, team, career, reputation, or future.

### Copy Pass diagnostic

Highlight every product feature. If the copy does not explicitly answer “so what?” after each one, it needs benefit translation.

## 7. Proof: Make Claims Believable

Claims create desire; proof creates conviction. The more ambitious the promise, the more proof the copy needs.

### Proof devices

- Testimonials and customer quotes.
- Case studies with before/after detail.
- Specific facts, figures, and measurable results.
- Demonstrations, samples, screenshots, photos, or walkthroughs.
- Guarantees, trials, or transparent terms.
- Credentials, awards, history, process, or expert validation.
- Comparisons against alternatives.
- Behind-the-scenes explanations that show how the result is produced.

### Specificity rules

- Specific numbers beat vague claims.
- Concrete nouns beat abstract adjectives.
- Demonstrated outcomes beat self-praise.
- Customer language beats company language.
- Facts should support the selling point, not decorate the page.

### Proof checklist

- What is the biggest claim in the copy?
- Is there proof next to or soon after that claim?
- Are testimonials specific rather than generic praise?
- Does the case study show a credible situation, action, and result?
- Are numbers rounded into vagueness when exact figures would be stronger?
- Are there unsupported superlatives such as “leading,” “unique,” “best,” or “world-class”?

## 8. Readability: Make the Copy Easy to Keep Reading

Maslen’s readability lessons are practical: write as you speak, use short words when they work, keep sentences manageable, use space, and design for understanding rather than impact.

### Plain-English principles

- Prefer familiar words over inflated ones.
- Write in a conversational tone suitable for the audience.
- Keep sentences varied but generally short.
- Use active verbs.
- Avoid clichés and hackneyed phrases.
- Use grammar and punctuation to clarify meaning, not to show off.
- Do not let wordplay obscure the proposition.

### Layout principles

- Use short paragraphs.
- Add cross-heads/subheads to help scanning.
- Use bullets for lists and proof points.
- Give the reader white space.
- Use type size and contrast that make reading comfortable.
- Put important information where gravity takes the eye: early, high, and clear.
- Online, skip unnecessary intros and make the page personal, direct, and scannable.

### Editing checklist

- Can a tired, distracted reader understand this on the first pass?
- Is every sentence doing a selling job?
- Are there long words where short words would work better?
- Are adjectives earning their place?
- Are clichés replaced by specific images or facts?
- Are paragraphs visually inviting?
- Are subheads meaningful enough to tell the story by themselves?

## 9. Objections: Sell to Skeptics, Not Believers

Good copy anticipates resistance. Readers ask “What’s the catch?” even when they want the result.

### Common objections

- “It’s too expensive.”
- “It won’t work for me.”
- “It sounds complicated.”
- “I don’t have time.”
- “I don’t trust you.”
- “I can do this myself.”
- “I’m not ready.”
- “There are hidden costs.”
- “Changing will be risky.”
- “I need approval.”

### Objection-handling methods

- Put the objection in the headline: “without hidden charges,” “without giving up…”
- Reframe price around value, cost of inaction, longevity, savings, or outcome.
- Use proof from people like the reader.
- Explain the process simply.
- Offer guarantees, trials, demos, or low-commitment next steps.
- Correct false assumptions directly.
- Use FAQs to answer remaining doubts.
- Show what happens after they respond, reducing uncertainty.

### Price objection checklist

- Have we shown the cost of not solving the problem?
- Have we anchored the price against alternatives?
- Have we broken the price into a smaller unit if useful?
- Have we shown durability, ROI, savings, or risk reduction?
- Have we avoided apologizing for the price?

## 10. CTAs: Ask Clearly and Confidently

The call to action is the close. It deserves as much craft as the headline.

### CTA principles

- Write the CTA before drafting to clarify the goal.
- Use a specific verb: call, book, download, order, reserve, compare, start, reply.
- Tell the reader exactly what happens next.
- Use assumptive language when appropriate: “When you reply…” rather than “If you reply…”
- Repeat CTAs after major persuasive sections.
- Reduce friction around forms, order pages, and response mechanisms.
- Do not add unnecessary fields or choices at the point of action.

### CTA formula

**Action + value + reassurance + immediacy**

Examples of structure:

- “Book your free consultation and find out which option fits your goals.”
- “Download the guide now and use the checklist before your next campaign.”
- “Reply by Friday to claim the bonus before it closes.”

### CTA review questions

- Is there one primary action?
- Is the next step obvious without scrolling or rereading?
- Does the CTA repeat the value, not just the mechanics?
- Is there a reason to act now?
- Have we removed avoidable form/order friction?

## 11. Direct Response Testing: Improve by Testing, Not Guessing

The book’s direct-response bias is clear: copy exists to generate results, so test the variables that affect response.

### What to test

- Headline angle: benefit, pain, curiosity, offer, objection, audience callout.
- Offer: discount vs bonus, free gift vs savings, trial vs consultation.
- CTA wording and placement.
- Lead/opening style: direct, story, question, problem, aspiration.
- Length: short vs long copy depending on complexity and commitment.
- Proof: testimonial, case study, data, demonstration, guarantee.
- Format: email, landing page, postcard, letter, advertorial, brochure, insert.
- Personalization: name, segment, role, industry, past behavior.
- Design/readability: subheads, type size, white space, images, order form friction.

### Testing rules

- Test one meaningful variable at a time when possible.
- Start with high-leverage variables: headline, offer, list/audience, CTA.
- Measure the action that matters, not vanity engagement alone.
- Keep learning records: hypothesis, version, audience, result, interpretation.
- Do not assume B2B buyers are immune to offers, urgency, emotion, or clear selling.
- Use results to refine the next test, not to declare permanent truth.

### Long copy vs short copy

Use longer copy when:

- The product is expensive, complex, unfamiliar, risky, or high-commitment.
- The reader needs education, proof, comparison, and objection handling.

Use shorter copy when:

- The reader already understands the offer.
- The action is low-risk or immediate.
- The channel demands speed.

The rule is not “long” or “short.” The rule is: give the reader enough information to act, and no more friction than necessary.

## 12. Channel and Format Notes

### Email and subject lines

- Treat the subject line like a headline: promise relevance, curiosity, or value.
- Get to the point quickly.
- Make the email feel personal, not broadcast-blasted.
- Use one main action.
- Repeat the offer and CTA clearly.

### Web and landing pages

- Make the page personal and scannable.
- Put the core value proposition high on the page.
- Use subheads that carry the sales argument.
- Cut “skip intro” style delays and decorative openings.
- Optimize first for customer understanding, then for search or internal stakeholders.

### Direct mail and physical formats

- Format can create attention: postcards, unusual sizes, inserts, envelopes, reply devices.
- Every component should sell: outer, letter, brochure, order form, reply envelope.
- Do not sabotage response with confusing forms or excess required information.

### Images and design

- Use images the reader identifies with, not generic stock clichés.
- Design should increase understanding, not merely visual impact.
- Copy and design must cooperate: subheads, hierarchy, type size, white space, and CTA visibility all affect persuasion.

## 13. Copy Pass Review Scorecard

Use this as a fast audit.

### Reader fit

- [ ] The copy is written to one clear reader.
- [ ] It uses reader language, not internal language.
- [ ] It addresses a real motivation, fear, pain, or aspiration.

### Headline and opening

- [ ] The headline contains a clear reason to read.
- [ ] The opening starts with the reader, not the company.
- [ ] The copy reaches the selling point quickly.

### Offer and benefits

- [ ] The offer is clear and visible.
- [ ] Features are translated into benefits.
- [ ] The strongest benefit is prominent.
- [ ] There is a reason to act now.

### Proof and objections

- [ ] Claims are backed by proof.
- [ ] Specific numbers/facts are used where useful.
- [ ] Testimonials or case studies are concrete.
- [ ] Major objections are anticipated and answered.

### Readability

- [ ] Sentences and paragraphs are easy to read.
- [ ] Subheads help scanners follow the argument.
- [ ] Clichés, jargon, and inflated words are removed.
- [ ] Design choices support comprehension.

### CTA and response

- [ ] There is one primary CTA.
- [ ] The CTA uses a clear verb.
- [ ] The reader knows what happens next.
- [ ] Forms/order steps are as frictionless as possible.

### Testing

- [ ] There is a clear hypothesis for what might improve response.
- [ ] The highest-leverage variable is identified.
- [ ] Results can be measured against the desired action.

## 14. Common Rewrite Moves

When copy feels weak, apply these moves:

- Replace company-first claims with reader-first benefits.
- Move the main promise or offer closer to the top.
- Turn abstract adjectives into concrete facts.
- Add proof after every major claim.
- Convert features into “so you can…” benefits.
- Add or sharpen urgency/limitation.
- Rewrite the headline to include self-interest or objection handling.
- Cut the first paragraph if it delays the argument.
- Break dense copy into subheads, bullets, and shorter paragraphs.
- Replace passive CTAs with specific next-step commands.
- Add an FAQ section for objections.
- Test a stronger offer before rewriting everything.

---

## 15. Compressed Principles From the Book

- Copy is selling in print or pixels.
- Talk to one person.
- Make it about “you,” not “we.”
- Find existing motivations; do not invent them.
- Benefits need proof.
- Offers work in B2B as well as B2C.
- Specificity beats puffery.
- Clarity beats cleverness.
- Long copy works when the reader needs more reasons to buy.
- Short copy works when the next step is obvious and low-friction.
- Objections belong in the copy, not outside it.
- The CTA is the close; write it deliberately.
- Design and readability are part of persuasion.
- Test because response is the final judge.
