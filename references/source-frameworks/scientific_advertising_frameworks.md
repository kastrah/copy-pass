# Scientific Advertising — Copywriting Frameworks

**Source:** Claude C. Hopkins, *Scientific Advertising*.
**Focus:** reusable public principles for measurable, service-led advertising.

## Core thesis
Advertising is “salesmanship in print” governed by tested laws, not guesswork. Judge copy by cost per customer, cost per sale, and repeated test results—not opinions, style, awards, or internal preference.

## 1. Scientific advertising method
- Start with a hypothesis: offer, headline, claim, proof, audience, price, media, or CTA.
- Run a small, traceable test before committing large spend.
- Track results with keyed ads, coupons, reply mechanisms, unique offers, or market-by-market tests.
- Compare alternatives on business outcomes: cost per inquiry, cost per customer, cost per dollar of sale, repeat purchase.
- Promote winners; discard losers; record learnings as principles.

**Checklist**
- What single result will prove this ad worked?
- How will responses be traced?
- What is the control version?
- What variable is being tested?
- Does the metric measure buyers, not just curiosity?

## 2. Advertising = multiplied salesmanship
Treat every ad as a salesperson speaking to one prospect.

**Rules**
- Write to persuade one person, not to impress a crowd.
- Ask: “Would this help a good salesperson close the sale?”
- Avoid cleverness that would sound artificial in person.
- Every line must serve the sale.
- Because one ad reaches many people, mediocre copy multiplies waste.

## 3. Offer service before asking for a sale
Hopkins argues the best ads often do not begin by demanding purchase; they offer useful service, samples, information, demonstrations, or help.

**Formula**
1. Identify the prospect’s need.
2. Offer help or proof with low friction.
3. Let the product demonstrate value.
4. Convert interest into purchase.

**Examples of service offers**
- Free sample.
- Demonstration.
- Educational booklet.
- Trial package.
- Useful diagnostic, recipe, guide, or comparison.

## 4. Headline as audience selector
The headline’s job is not merely attention; it selects the right people and tells them why to continue.

**Headline checklist**
- Does it call out the right prospect or problem?
- Does it promise a benefit, news, saving, solution, or useful information?
- Is it specific enough to separate buyers from non-buyers?
- Can it be tested against other headlines?
- Would the target reader instantly know “this is for me”?

## 5. Specific claims beat general claims
Generalities are weak; specifics create belief.

**Convert vague to specific**
- “High quality” → state the ingredient, process, test, source, guarantee, or measurable result.
- “Saves money” → quantify the saving.
- “Fast” → state the time.
- “Popular” → state the number of users, stores, years, awards, or repeat buyers.

**Rule:** If a claim is worth making, make it definite.

## 6. Tell the full story
Do not assume prospects already know why to buy. A complete selling story can outperform a short, incomplete ad when the product requires explanation.

**Full-story structure**
1. Who the product is for.
2. The problem or desire.
3. The product’s unique mechanism or advantage.
4. Specific proof.
5. Objections answered.
6. Offer, sample, trial, or guarantee.
7. Clear action step.

## 7. Use psychology ethically
Advertising should account for human motives and friction.

**Principles**
- Curiosity can draw attention, but must connect to the sale.
- People value what appears scarce, earned, or personalized.
- Free offers work best when they feel meaningful, not indiscriminate.
- A request for action should feel easy and natural.
- Copy should reduce fear, uncertainty, and effort.

## 8. Samples and trials as proof
Samples can be powerful when the product experience sells better than claims.

**Sample framework**
- Offer the sample only to likely prospects.
- Make redemption traceable.
- Use the sample to prove the central claim.
- Follow up with a purchase path.
- Compare sample cost against acquired customers, not just requests.

## 9. Strategy before copy
Before writing, understand the business situation.

**Strategy questions**
- What is the market size and maturity?
- What alternatives does the prospect already use?
- How entrenched are competitors?
- What claim, offer, or mechanism can be owned?
- Is distribution ready to capture demand?
- What price, margin, and repeat purchase pattern can support advertising?

## 10. Information gathering
Good copy is built from facts.

**Research sources**
- Product tests and demonstrations.
- Manufacturing process.
- Customer interviews.
- Salespeople and dealers.
- Competitor claims.
- Objections and reasons for non-purchase.
- Unusual facts hidden inside the product or company.

**Rule:** The copywriter’s job is often to discover the selling fact, not invent one.

## 11. Avoid art for art’s sake
Visuals, layout, humor, and design must help sell.

**Use visuals when they:**
- Demonstrate the product.
- Show the result.
- Clarify the claim.
- Attract the right prospect.
- Increase belief or desire.

**Avoid visuals when they:**
- Distract from the message.
- Attract non-buyers.
- Exist only to entertain.
- Make the ad harder to understand.

## 12. Negative advertising usually wastes space
Tell people what to do and what benefit they get—not merely what to avoid.

**Rewrite pattern**
- Negative: “Don’t suffer from X.”
- Positive: “Get Y result with Z method.”

## 13. Names can help or hinder
A product name is part of the advertisement.

**Helpful names**
- Suggest the benefit, category, use, or distinction.
- Are easy to remember and repeat.
- Avoid confusion with competitors.
- Support the central selling promise.

## 14. Letter-writing principle
Direct letters and ads follow the same law: personal, specific, service-led persuasion.

**Letter framework**
1. Personal opening tied to the reader’s interest.
2. Clear reason for writing.
3. Specific benefit or offer.
4. Proof.
5. Risk reversal or sample/trial.
6. Direct response instruction.

## 15. Good business as the foundation
Advertising cannot permanently overcome a weak product, poor value, bad service, or lack of distribution. The strongest advertising amplifies a real advantage and a sound business model.

## Practical Hopkins copy checklist
- Is this salesmanship, not showmanship?
- Is the offer useful to the prospect?
- Is the claim specific and provable?
- Is the headline selecting the right audience?
- Have we told enough of the story to sell?
- Is every element measurable?
- Are we testing before scaling?
- Are we judging by customers and sales, not applause?
