# Reality in Advertising — Copywriting Frameworks

**Source:** Rosser Reeves, *Reality in Advertising*.
**Focus:** reusable public principles for message discipline, USP development, and measurable advertising effectiveness.

## Core thesis
Advertising should be judged by whether it moves people toward buying. Beautiful, entertaining, or memorable advertising can still fail if people remember the wrong thing or if the remembered message does not create sales.

## 1. The USP framework
A strong campaign needs a **Unique Selling Proposition**.

**Three requirements**
1. **Proposition:** The ad must promise a clear benefit: “Buy this product and you will get this specific advantage.”
2. **Uniqueness:** The proposition must be something competitors either cannot offer or are not currently offering.
3. **Selling power:** The proposition must be strong enough to move mass numbers of people—i.e., pull new customers to the product.

**USP test**
- What exactly does the customer get?
- Is the claim meaningfully different in the category?
- Is it important enough to change behavior?
- Can it be stated simply and repeatedly?
- Can the brand credibly own it?

## 2. Proposition over puffery
Reeves attacks “show-window advertising”: attractive words, images, slogans, and atmosphere with no sales proposition.

**Puffery signals**
- Generic adjectives: delicious, smooth, premium, trusted, refreshing, modern.
- Category clichés competitors could also say.
- Brand mood without a reason to buy.
- Visual devices remembered more than the product claim.
- Slogans that do not complete the sentence: “Buy this because…”

**Rewrite pattern**
- Puffery: “The refreshing choice.”
- Proposition: “Refreshment that stays colder for 30 minutes because of [specific feature].”

## 3. Penetration and usage pull
Reeves separates two questions:

- **Penetration:** How many people remember the advertising and can correctly state its message?
- **Usage pull:** Among people who remember the advertising, how many more buy or use the product compared with people who do not remember it?

**Effectiveness formula**
- Advertising effect = message penetration × sales pull.
- High penetration with no usage pull means people remember but do not buy.
- High usage pull with low penetration means the message sells but is not reaching enough people.
- Negative usage pull means the advertising may repel buyers.

## 4. The pulled vs. unpulled audit
Split the market into two groups:

1. People who do not know or remember the current advertising.
2. People who do remember it and can say what it communicates.

Then compare purchase or usage rates. The difference reveals whether the copy is pulling customers over.

**Audit questions**
- What percentage remembers the campaign?
- What do they remember: the selling message or a device?
- Do rememberers buy more than non-rememberers?
- If not, is the proposition weak, hidden, irrelevant, or repellent?

## 5. Message discipline and campaign life
A good campaign should not be changed merely because the advertiser or agency is tired of it. The public forgets quickly; penetration leaks away unless reinforced.

**Rules**
- Do not abandon a working USP prematurely.
- Wear-out should be measured in the market, not in the conference room.
- If most prospects still do not know the message, the campaign is not worn out.
- Consistent repetition builds ownership and memory.
- A competitor with a consistent message can overtake a brand that keeps changing campaigns.

## 6. The leaking bucket principle
Consumer memory decays. Advertising must keep refilling the bucket.

**Implications**
- Maintain pressure behind a proven message.
- Avoid needless changes that discard accumulated penetration.
- Track whether memory is rising, stable, or falling.
- Treat a campaign as an asset that can accumulate value over time.

## 7. Copy leverage
Small differences in message clarity and strength can create large differences in sales because the same media dollars are multiplied across millions of exposures.

**Leverage points**
- Sharper USP.
- Stronger headline promise.
- More direct demonstration.
- Better proof.
- Removing distracting devices.
- Placing the main claim where it cannot be missed.

## 8. Three roads to a USP
When products appear similar, Reeves identifies three broad ways to create or find a USP:

1. **Find a real product difference:** uncover a meaningful feature, process, ingredient, result, or use already present.
2. **Create a product difference:** improve or change the product so the advertising has something real to sell.
3. **Preempt a claim:** be first to strongly claim a relevant benefit that others may technically share but have not advertised.

**Warning:** Preempted claims can work, but a real product advantage is stronger and more defensible.

## 9. Avoid deceptive differentials
Do not build a campaign on a difference too small for consumers to experience or care about.

**Valid difference checklist**
- Is it real?
- Is it perceptible or credibly consequential?
- Does it matter to the buying decision?
- Can it be explained simply?
- Does it support a benefit, not merely a technical distinction?

## 10. Ownable claim and preemption
A brand can sometimes “own” a claim by being first to attach it to memory at scale.

**Preemption checklist**
- Competitors are not already known for the claim.
- The claim matters to buyers.
- The brand can repeat it consistently.
- The media budget can build penetration before competitors respond.
- The claim will not accidentally advertise the market leader.

## 11. Beware the vampire device
A dramatic device, character, joke, image, or production trick can suck attention away from the selling message.

**Device test**
- Do people remember the brand?
- Do they remember the proposition?
- Does the device dramatize the USP or compete with it?
- Could the device be removed while leaving the sales idea stronger?

## 12. Brand image is not a substitute for sell
Image can matter, but image without a proposition often produces familiarity without persuasion. A brand impression should grow out of the selling claim, not replace it.

**Better sequence**
1. Establish the sales proposition.
2. Dramatize it consistently.
3. Let brand image accumulate from repeated proof and benefit.

## 13. Frequency vs. reach question
The key media question is not only “how many impressions?” but “how many people will receive and retain the selling message?”

**Planning questions**
- Is the message simple enough to penetrate with available frequency?
- Is it better to reach more people lightly or fewer people repeatedly?
- Are competitors crowding the same memory space?
- Does the campaign need continuity to prevent memory decay?

## 14. Sales reality before advertising blame
Sales can rise or fall for reasons outside copy: product quality, price, distribution, sales force, competitor deals, category trends, or supply problems. Advertising must be audited against its own job: message penetration and sales pull.

## 15. Reeves campaign checklist
- Is there a clear USP?
- Is the USP stated as a customer benefit?
- Is it unique or preemptable?
- Is it strong enough to move buyers?
- Can prospects repeat the message after exposure?
- Are people who remember the message more likely to buy?
- Are we avoiding puffery and empty image?
- Are visuals and devices serving the proposition?
- Are we protecting accumulated campaign memory?
- Are we measuring penetration and usage pull over time?

## Compact copy formula
**USP → proof/dramatization → repetition → penetration → usage pull → sales.**

If any link is weak, improve the message before increasing spend.
